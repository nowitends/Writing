# Ideas for the introduction

* We should explain in the introduction why this script exists and what role it should play in the course.

* The script is not meant to be an encyclopedia of physics. It should not try to cover all of mechanics, waves, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, or modern physics. Its purpose is to select a small but meaningful part of physics and present it carefully enough that students can understand how physical reasoning works.

* One function of the script is practical: it should collect the minimum amount of knowledge needed to pass the course. However, this should not mean reducing physics to a list of formulas. The minimum should include concepts, methods, assumptions, and simple models, not only final equations.

* The students are not assumed to be highly advanced mathematically or physically. For this reason, the text should avoid unnecessary formalism. At the same time, it should not trivialize physics. Whenever possible, we should show the real structure of the subject: definitions, assumptions, idealizations, models, consequences, and limits of applicability.

* The main goal is to teach students how physicists think. Physics should be presented as a method of describing the world through models, measurements, approximations, and mathematical reasoning.

* The script should emphasize that physical laws are not isolated formulas. A formula has meaning only when we know what the symbols represent, what assumptions were made, what system is being described, what has been neglected, and in what range the formula can be used.

* We should repeatedly show the difference between:
  - a real physical situation,
  - an idealized physical model,
  - a mathematical description of that model,
  - a prediction obtained from the model,
  - and comparison with observation or experiment.

* The introduction should make clear that simplification is not the same as falsification. In physics we often simplify reality deliberately: we neglect air resistance, treat a body as a point mass, assume a perfectly rigid body, a frictionless surface, a small oscillation, or a linear response. These simplifications are acceptable only when we understand why they are useful and when they stop being valid.

* A central theme of the notes should be methodology:
  - how to define a physical system,
  - how to choose variables,
  - how to identify relevant quantities,
  - how to make assumptions explicit,
  - how to build a model,
  - how to derive consequences,
  - how to check dimensions and units,
  - how to interpret the result physically.

* The notes should teach students to read equations. An equation should not be treated as a computational recipe only. Students should learn to ask: What changes if this variable increases? What remains constant? What is proportional to what? What are the limiting cases? Does the result make physical sense?

* We should include many simple but meaningful examples. The examples should not be artificially difficult computationally. Their purpose is to show physical reasoning: how a conclusion follows from a model, not how to perform long algebraic manipulations.

* Mathematics should be used honestly, but economically. Derivatives, integrals, vectors, functions, and differential equations should appear when they are genuinely useful. They should be connected with physical meaning: derivative as rate of change, integral as accumulation, vector as quantity with direction, differential equation as a law describing evolution.

* The script should help students understand the role of units and dimensional analysis. Checking units is one of the simplest ways to detect nonsense and one of the first steps toward independent physical thinking.

* We should show that physical quantities are defined operationally whenever possible. For example, time, position, velocity, acceleration, force, energy, momentum, frequency, wavelength, and temperature should not appear only as symbols but as quantities connected with measurement and interpretation.

* The introduction should also say that physics is not only about obtaining exact answers. Many physical problems are solved approximately. Approximation, order-of-magnitude reasoning, limiting cases, and qualitative interpretation are part of real physics.

* The notes should distinguish between calculation and understanding. A student may be able to substitute numbers into a formula without understanding the model. The aim of the course is to move from substitution to interpretation.

* The script should include comments about common misconceptions. For example:
  - force is not the same as velocity,
  - acceleration can be nonzero even when speed is constant,
  - zero net force does not mean zero velocity,
  - energy conservation depends on the system and interactions considered,
  - mathematical idealizations are not literal descriptions of reality.

* The text should be written in a way that supports lectures. It should not replace the lecture completely, but it should give students a stable reference: definitions, key ideas, examples, and explanations that they can return to after class.

* The tone should be serious but accessible. We should not pretend that physics is easier than it is, but we should also not make the first contact with physics unnecessarily intimidating.

* The introduction should motivate the choice of topics. Even if we cover only selected parts of physics, they should be chosen because they introduce general methods: motion, forces, conservation laws, oscillations, waves, fields, energy, and modelling.

* The script should present physics as a coherent way of thinking, not as a disconnected collection of school topics. Mechanics, waves, and other parts of physics should be linked by recurring ideas: state of a system, change in time, interaction, conservation, equilibrium, stability, linearization, and response to disturbance.

* We should make clear that passing the course is one goal, but not the only one. A more important goal is to give students enough conceptual and methodological foundation to understand future technical, mathematical, or scientific material involving physical models.

## Central structure of the notes

A useful recurring pattern for the whole script should be:

```text
real situation -> idealized model -> mathematical description -> prediction -> interpretation/check
```

This pattern should appear explicitly in the introduction and then return naturally in later chapters. It helps students see that physics is not merely a collection of formulas, but a disciplined way of moving from reality to a model, from a model to mathematics, and from mathematics back to physical interpretation.
