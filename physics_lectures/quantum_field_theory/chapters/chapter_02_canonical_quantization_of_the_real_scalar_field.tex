\chapter{Canonical Quantization of the Real Scalar Field}

\begin{remarkbox}
Chapter 1 explained why a free field should be thought of as a collection of
oscillators. This chapter makes that statement precise. We impose canonical
commutation relations on the field and its conjugate momentum, derive the
standard mode expansion, and recover the Fock-space interpretation from the
field algebra itself.
\end{remarkbox}

\section{From the Oscillator Picture to Field Operators}

The previous chapter started from normal modes. It argued that a free scalar
field is a collection of harmonic oscillators labelled by momentum. That is the
right intuition, but it is not yet the full canonical quantization of a field.

The full canonical construction begins before the normal-mode expansion. It
starts with the classical field \(\phi(t,\vect{x})\), its conjugate momentum
\(\pi(t,\vect{x})\), and their Poisson brackets. Quantization then promotes
\(\phi\) and \(\pi\) to operators and replaces the Poisson brackets by
commutators.

The logic of this chapter is therefore
\[
    \text{classical field variables}
    \longrightarrow
    \text{equal-time commutators}
    \longrightarrow
    \text{operator mode expansion}
    \longrightarrow
    \text{creation and annihilation operators}
    \longrightarrow
    \text{Fock space}.
\]
This is the formal version of the oscillator picture developed in Chapter 1.

\section{Classical Starting Point}

We use units \(c=\hbar=1\) and the metric convention \((+,-,-,-)\). The free
real scalar field is described by the Lagrangian density
\[
    \mathcal{L}
    = \frac{1}{2}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi
      - \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2
    = \frac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2
      - \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\phi)^2
      - \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2.
\]
The conjugate momentum is
\[
    \pi(t,\vect{x})
    = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\dot\phi(t,\vect{x})}
    = \dot\phi(t,\vect{x}).
\]
The Euler--Lagrange equation is the Klein--Gordon equation,
\[
    (\Box+m^2)\phi(x)=0,
\]
where \(x=(t,\vect{x})\). In components this reads
\[
    (\partial_t^2-\nabla^2+m^2)\phi(t,\vect{x})=0.
\]

The classical Hamiltonian density is
\[
    \mathcal{H}
    = \frac{1}{2}\pi^2
      + \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\phi)^2
      + \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2,
\]
so the Hamiltonian is
\[
    H
    = \int \dd^3x\,
    \left(
        \frac{1}{2}\pi^2
        + \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\phi)^2
        + \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2
    \right).
\]
This is the continuum field-theoretic analogue of the oscillator Hamiltonian
\(p^2/2+\omega^2q^2/2\).

\section{Equal-Time Commutation Relations}

The classical canonical Poisson bracket is
\[
    \{\phi(t,\vect{x}),\pi(t,\vect{y})\}_{\mathrm{PB}}
    = \delta^{(3)}(\vect{x}-\vect{y}).
\]
Canonical quantization promotes \(\phi\) and \(\pi\) to operators
\(\hat\phi\) and \(\hat\pi\), and imposes
\[
    [\hat\phi(t,\vect{x}),\hat\pi(t,\vect{y})]
    = i\delta^{(3)}(\vect{x}-\vect{y}).
\]
The remaining equal-time commutators vanish:
\[
    [\hat\phi(t,\vect{x}),\hat\phi(t,\vect{y})]=0,
    \qquad
    [\hat\pi(t,\vect{x}),\hat\pi(t,\vect{y})]=0.
\]
These relations are the field version of
\[
    [\hat q,\hat p]=i.
\]
Instead of one coordinate and one momentum, there is one canonical pair at every
point of space.

\begin{definitionbox}
The \emph{equal-time canonical commutation relations} are the basic operator
relations between \(\hat\phi\) and \(\hat\pi\) at a fixed time. For bosonic
fields they are commutators; for fermionic fields they will be replaced by
anticommutators.
\end{definitionbox}

\section{Finite Box and Continuum Normalization}

Chapter 1 used a finite box to keep the momentum labels discrete. In a finite
box, one writes sums over momenta and Kronecker deltas. In infinite volume, one
uses integrals and Dirac deltas. The replacement rules are schematically
\[
    \frac{1}{V}\sum_{\vect{k}}
    \longrightarrow
    \int\frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3},
    \qquad
    V\delta_{\vect{k},\vect{p}}
    \longrightarrow
    (2\pi)^3\delta^{(3)}(\vect{k}-\vect{p}).
\]
The continuum formulas below use the second convention. They look slightly more
complicated than finite-box formulas, but they are the standard relativistic
normalization used in scattering theory.

\section{Mode Expansion of the Field Operator}

The quantum field operator is expanded in positive- and negative-frequency
solutions of the Klein--Gordon equation:
\[
    \hat\phi(t,\vect{x})
    = \int \frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}
      \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\omega_{\vect{k}}}}
      \left(
          \hat a_{\vect{k}}
          e^{-i\omega_{\vect{k}}t+i\vect{k}\cdot\vect{x}}
          +
          \hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger
          e^{i\omega_{\vect{k}}t-i\vect{k}\cdot\vect{x}}
      \right),
\]
where
\[
    \omega_{\vect{k}}=\sqrt{\vect{k}^{\,2}+m^2}.
\]
Since the field is real, \(\hat\phi\) is Hermitian:
\[
    \hat\phi(t,\vect{x})^\dagger=\hat\phi(t,\vect{x}).
\]
The appearance of both \(\hat a_{\vect{k}}\) and
\(\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger\) is the operator version of the reality condition
on a classical real field.

The conjugate momentum operator is
\[
    \hat\pi(t,\vect{x})
    = \partial_t\hat\phi(t,\vect{x}),
\]
therefore
\[
    \hat\pi(t,\vect{x})
    = -i\int \frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}
      \sqrt{\frac{\omega_{\vect{k}}}{2}}
      \left(
          \hat a_{\vect{k}}
          e^{-i\omega_{\vect{k}}t+i\vect{k}\cdot\vect{x}}
          -
          \hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger
          e^{i\omega_{\vect{k}}t-i\vect{k}\cdot\vect{x}}
      \right).
\]
The normalization factors are chosen so that the equal-time field commutators
are satisfied.

\section{Ladder-Operator Algebra}

The canonical field commutators imply
\[
    [\hat a_{\vect{k}},\hat a_{\vect{p}}^\dagger]
    = (2\pi)^3\delta^{(3)}(\vect{k}-\vect{p}),
\]
with
\[
    [\hat a_{\vect{k}},\hat a_{\vect{p}}]=0,
    \qquad
    [\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger,\hat a_{\vect{p}}^\dagger]=0.
\]
Conversely, these ladder-operator relations imply the equal-time commutators of
\(\hat\phi\) and \(\hat\pi\). This is the precise connection between the
canonical field algebra and the oscillator algebra of Chapter 1.

The operator \(\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger\) creates a quantum in the momentum
mode \(\vect{k}\), while \(\hat a_{\vect{k}}\) annihilates such a quantum. For
the real scalar field there is only one kind of neutral scalar quantum.

\section{Fock Space Revisited}

The free-field vacuum is defined by
\[
    \hat a_{\vect{k}}|0\rangle=0
    \qquad \text{for every }\vect{k}.
\]
A one-particle momentum eigenstate is
\[
    |\vect{k}\rangle
    = \hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger|0\rangle.
\]
A two-particle state is
\[
    |\vect{k}_1,\vect{k}_2\rangle
    = \hat a_{\vect{k}_1}^\dagger
      \hat a_{\vect{k}_2}^\dagger|0\rangle,
\]
up to normalization. Since the creation operators commute, the scalar quanta are
bosons.

A realistic one-particle state is usually a wave packet rather than a single
momentum eigenstate:
\[
    |f\rangle
    = \int\frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,f(\vect{k})
      \hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger|0\rangle.
\]
The function \(f(\vect{k})\) describes the momentum profile of the state. This
is the first place where the particle idea becomes more refined than the simple
phrase ``one quantum of one mode.''

\section{Hamiltonian and Momentum Operators}

The quantum Hamiltonian is obtained from the classical Hamiltonian by replacing
\(\phi\) and \(\pi\) by field operators:
\[
    \hat H
    = \int \dd^3x\,
    \left(
        \frac{1}{2}\hat\pi^2
        + \frac{1}{2}(\nabla\hat\phi)^2
        + \frac{1}{2}m^2\hat\phi^{\,2}
    \right).
\]
Substituting the mode expansion gives the formal expression
\[
    \hat H
    = \int \frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,
      \omega_{\vect{k}}
      \left(
          \hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger\hat a_{\vect{k}}
          + \frac{1}{2}(2\pi)^3\delta^{(3)}(0)
      \right).
\]
The first term counts excitations; the second term is the continuum notation for
the sum of oscillator zero-point energies.

The spatial momentum operator is
\[
    \hat{\vect{P}}
    = \int \dd^3x\,\hat\pi\nabla\hat\phi.
\]
With the same ordering convention, it becomes
\[
    \hat{\vect{P}}
    = \int \frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,
      \vect{k}\,\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger\hat a_{\vect{k}}.
\]
Thus
\[
    \hat H\,\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger|0\rangle
    = \omega_{\vect{k}}\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger|0\rangle
    \quad \text{above the vacuum energy},
\]
and
\[
    \hat{\vect{P}}\,\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger|0\rangle
    = \vect{k}\,\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger|0\rangle.
\]
The dispersion relation is
\[
    \omega_{\vect{k}}^2=\vect{k}^{\,2}+m^2.
\]
This is why \(m\) is interpreted as the mass of the scalar quantum.

\section{Normal Ordering}

The vacuum-energy term in \(\hat H\) is infinite in the continuum limit. For the
free theory, it is common to define the normal-ordered Hamiltonian by moving all
creation operators to the left of all annihilation operators:
\[
    :\hat a_{\vect{k}}\hat a_{\vect{p}}^\dagger:
    = \hat a_{\vect{p}}^\dagger\hat a_{\vect{k}}.
\]
Then
\[
    :\hat H:
    = \int \frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,
      \omega_{\vect{k}}\hat a_{\vect{k}}^\dagger\hat a_{\vect{k}},
\]
and
\[
    \langle0|:\hat H:|0\rangle=0.
\]
Normal ordering removes the constant free vacuum energy from this Hamiltonian.
It does not remove all divergences of interacting quantum field theory. It is a
useful convention at the free-field level, not a substitute for
renormalization.

\section{Zero-Point Energy}

In a finite box, the vacuum energy of the free scalar field is
\[
    E_0
    = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\vect{k}}\omega_{\vect{k}}.
\]
In infinite-volume notation this becomes
\[
    E_0
    = \frac{V}{2}\int\frac{\dd^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\,\omega_{\vect{k}}.
\]
The integral diverges at large momentum. The divergence is a reminder that a
field has infinitely many short-distance degrees of freedom.

For many non-gravitational scattering problems, only energy differences above
the free vacuum matter, and normal ordering is sufficient. In contexts where
vacuum energy gravitates, or where the vacuum itself changes, the issue becomes
more subtle. The present course will return to this point when discussing
renormalization and vacuum structure.

\section{Fields as Operator-Valued Distributions}

The notation \(\hat\phi(x)\) suggests an operator at a point. This notation is
useful, but local quantum fields are more accurately understood as
operator-valued distributions. They become well-defined only after smearing with
test functions.

At fixed time, a smeared field is
\[
    \hat\phi(f)
    = \int\dd^3x\,f(\vect{x})\hat\phi(t,\vect{x}).
\]
Products such as \(\hat\phi(x)^2\) at the same spacetime point are generally
singular and require a prescription. This is one of the structural reasons why
renormalization appears in interacting field theory.

This point also clarifies the role of the mode expansion. The formula for
\(\hat\phi(t,\vect{x})\) is a compact way to encode how the field acts after it
has been smeared. It should not be treated as an ordinary function with operator
coefficients.

\section{Microcausality}

Relativistic quantum field theory must respect the causal structure of
spacetime. For a scalar field, the basic local causality condition is
\[
    [\hat\phi(x),\hat\phi(y)]=0
    \qquad \text{if}\qquad (x-y)^2<0.
\]
This condition is called \emph{microcausality}. It says that field observables
associated with spacelike separated regions commute.

For the free scalar field one can write
\[
    [\hat\phi(x),\hat\phi(y)]
    = i\Delta(x-y),
\]
where \(\Delta\) is the Pauli--Jordan commutator function. This function
vanishes outside the light cone. The equal-time relation
\[
    [\hat\phi(t,\vect{x}),\hat\phi(t,\vect{y})]=0
\]
is therefore only the simplest special case of the full spacetime causality
condition.

\begin{summarybox}
Canonical quantization begins with \(\hat\phi\) and \(\hat\pi\), not directly
with particles. The equal-time commutation relations determine the ladder
operator algebra in the mode expansion. The free-field vacuum and Fock space are
then recovered from that algebra, matching the oscillator picture of Chapter 1.
The new lessons are the precise continuum normalization, the Hamiltonian and
momentum operators, normal ordering, the distributional nature of fields, and
microcausality.
\end{summarybox}

\section{Chapter Checklist}

After reading this chapter, one should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item write the classical Lagrangian and Hamiltonian of the free real scalar
    field;
    \item derive the conjugate momentum \(\pi=\dot\phi\);
    \item state the equal-time canonical commutation relations;
    \item write the mode expansions of \(\hat\phi\) and \(\hat\pi\);
    \item derive the ladder-operator commutators from the canonical field
    algebra;
    \item construct the vacuum, one-particle states, wave packets, and
    many-particle states;
    \item express \(\hat H\) and \(\hat{\vect{P}}\) in terms of ladder
    operators;
    \item explain what normal ordering removes and what it does not remove;
    \item explain why fields are operator-valued distributions;
    \item state the microcausality condition for a scalar field.
\end{itemize}
