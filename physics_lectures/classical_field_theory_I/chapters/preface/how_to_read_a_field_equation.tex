\section{How to Read a Field Equation}

A field equation should be read locally first. A typical equation relates the
field and its derivatives at a point. For example, an equation of the form
\[
    \partial_\mu \partial^\mu \phi + m^2 \phi = 0
\]
states that the local curvature of the field configuration, in spacetime, is
related to the local value of the field itself.

Several questions should be asked whenever a new field equation appears.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item What is the unknown field?
    \item What is the domain: space, spacetime, or a curved manifold?
    \item Which derivatives appear, and what do they measure?
    \item What constants or parameters occur, and what are their dimensions?
    \item What initial or boundary data are needed?
    \item Does the equation come from an action principle?
    \item Which symmetries and conservation laws does it have?
\end{enumerate}

This habit prevents a common mistake: treating field equations as isolated
formulas. In physics, an equation is a compressed statement about a model. It
must be unpacked.

\begin{examplebox}
Maxwell's equations are not only four equations for \(\vect{E}\) and
\(\vect{B}\). They also encode locality, charge conservation, gauge structure,
propagation of electromagnetic waves, and the coupling between fields and
sources.
\end{examplebox}
