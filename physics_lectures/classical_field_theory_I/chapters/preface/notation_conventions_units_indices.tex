\section{Notation, Conventions, Units, and Index Language}

These notes use standard mathematical notation for fields and their derivatives.
Spatial vectors are written in boldface, for example \(\vect{x}\) and
\(\vect{E}\). Spacetime indices are usually Greek:
\[
    \mu,\nu,\rho,\sigma = 0,1,2,3.
\]
Spatial indices are usually Latin:
\[
    i,j,k = 1,2,3.
\]
Repeated upper and lower indices are summed when index notation is used.

The derivative \(\partial_\mu\) denotes differentiation with respect to the
coordinate \(x^\mu\). In relativistic notation, field equations are often
written so that their transformation properties under Lorentz transformations
are visible. When the metric is needed, the convention will be stated before it
is used.

Units are part of the physics. Sometimes constants such as \(c\), \(\hbar\), or
\(\varepsilon_0\) will be displayed explicitly to clarify dimensions. In more
theoretical sections, natural units may be used to simplify formulas. The text
will indicate when such simplifications are made.

\begin{remarkbox}
Index notation is a language, not a decoration. It records which objects are
scalars, vectors, covectors, tensors, and densities. A formula with indices
should be read together with its transformation meaning.
\end{remarkbox}
